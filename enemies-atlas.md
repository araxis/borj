# Shahnameh Enemy Atlas Ledger

Image: ![Enemy atlas](../../src/assets/atlases/enemies-atlas.png)

Grid: 5 columns x 6 rows. Each image cell follows the exact position below.

Source note: story references name the Ferdowsi Shahnameh episode/cycle used for the short and detailed descriptions. Rows marked "minor/brief figure" or "game composite" should not be treated as full independent Ferdowsi episodes.

| Position | Enemy / Opposing Figure | Ferdowsi Shahnameh Story Reference | Short Description | Shahnameh-Based Description |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Row 1, Col 1 | Zahhak / ضحاک | Zahhak, Kaveh, and Fereydun; Fereydun binds Zahhak on Mount Damavand | The serpent-shouldered tyrant whose reign follows Jamshid's fall. | Zahhak's identity comes directly from the Zahhak cycle: Ahriman's corruption, the two serpents growing from his shoulders, the killing of youths to feed them, Kaveh's revolt, and Fereydun's final binding of him in Damavand. His design should show royal power ruined by demonic hunger. |
| Row 1, Col 2 | Zahhak's Serpents / مارهای ضحاک | Zahhak, Kaveh, and Fereydun | The living curse on Zahhak's shoulders. | The serpents are not separate kings or warriors; they are the visible sign of Zahhak's tyranny. Their story reference is the same Zahhak episode, where the demand for human brains makes the horror of his rule immediate and personal. |
| Row 1, Col 3 | Div-e Sepid / دیو سپید | Rostam's Seven Labours: seventh labour in Mazandaran | The White Div defeated by Rostam to save Kay Kavus. | Div-e Sepid is the final Mazandaran threat in Rostam's rescue of Kay Kavus. Rostam defeats him in the cavern and uses the div's blood to heal the blinded king and army, so the image should show cave darkness, pale monstrous force, and the reversal of blindness. |
| Row 1, Col 4 | Arzhang Div / ارژنگ دیو | Rostam's Seven Labours: assault on Arzhang Div before Div-e Sepid | A Mazandaran div commander defeated by Rostam. | Arzhang Div belongs to the military side of the Mazandaran campaign: Rostam breaks through the div host before reaching Div-e Sepid. He should read as a demonic commander with banners and field command, not as a nameless creature. |
| Row 1, Col 5 | Akvan Div / اکوان دیو | Rostam and Akvan Div | A shape-shifting div who tries to outwit Rostam. | Akvan Div's story turns on trickery and reversal: he uproots the ground beneath Rostam and asks whether to throw him toward mountain or sea. Rostam survives by understanding the div's deceit, so the design should emphasize shifting form, wind, lifted earth, and cunning rather than brute force alone. |
| Row 2, Col 1 | Kharvazan Div / خروَزان دیو | Mazandaran div cycle; minor/uncertain figure in Ferdowsi tradition | A heavy lesser div used as a Mazandaran war-beast. | Kharvazan is treated here as a lesser div attached to the Mazandaran danger-world rather than as a full independent Shahnameh episode. His role should stay subordinate to the Seven Labours setting: weight, obstruction, and demonic pressure on the road. |
| Row 2, Col 2 | Olad Div / اولاد دیو | Rostam's Seven Labours: capture of Olad as guide to Mazandaran | The captured div-guide who leads Rostam through Mazandaran. | Olad is not simply a monster to kill. Rostam captures him and uses him as a guide toward Arzhang Div and Div-e Sepid. His image should show fear, knowledge of the land, and forced obedience after defeat. |
| Row 2, Col 3 | Azhdaha / اژدها | Rostam's Seven Labours: third labour, the dragon | The dragon that attacks Rostam while Rakhsh warns him. | The Seven Labours dragon episode belongs equally to Rostam and Rakhsh: the horse sees the hidden dragon and repeatedly wakes Rostam before the fight. The creature should show scale, sudden emergence, and the bond between champion and horse. |
| Row 2, Col 4 | Haftvad's Worm / کرم هفتواد | Ardashir and Haftvad; the Worm of Haftvad in the Sasanian section | The worm whose unnatural growth empowers Haftvad's house. | In the Ardashir story, Haftvad's worm begins as a strange household marvel and grows into a source of political power before Ardashir destroys it. Its design should feel like corruption spreading from a small omen into a fortress-threatening monster. |
| Row 2, Col 5 | White Elephant / پیل سپید | Youth of Rostam: Rostam kills the white elephant | The huge elephant slain by young Rostam. | The white elephant episode proves Rostam's strength before his greatest campaigns. The elephant should be monumental and battle-ready, because the story functions as an early sign that Rostam is not an ordinary youth. |
| Row 3, Col 1 | Mazandaran Sorceress / جادوگر مازندران | Rostam's Seven Labours: fourth labour, the sorceress/witch | A deceptive enchantress of Mazandaran. | The sorceress appears in Rostam's Mazandaran trials as a beautiful-seeming danger that reveals its monstrous nature under sacred pressure. The card should show the transformation from seduction to threat and the magical atmosphere of the fourth labour. |
| Row 3, Col 2 | Mazandaran Lion / شیر مازندران | Rostam's Seven Labours: first labour, the lion | The lion killed by Rakhsh while Rostam sleeps. | The first labour demonstrates Rakhsh's agency as much as Rostam's. A lion attacks the sleeping hero, and Rakhsh fights it off, so the image should show a raw wilderness assault and the horse's protective power. |
| Row 3, Col 3 | Afrasiab / افراسیاب | Siyavash in Turan; Kay Khosrow's war against Afrasiab | King of Turan and the central royal enemy of Iran. | Afrasiab is a king, not a monster. His major Shahnameh weight comes from receiving Siyavash, ordering or allowing his death under intrigue, and later being hunted by Kay Khosrow. His design should carry royal grandeur, fear of prophecy, and tragic political enmity. |
| Row 3, Col 4 | Garsivaz / گرسیوز | Siyavash in Turan; Garsivaz's accusations against Siyavash | Afrasiab's brother and the court intriguer behind Siyavash's death. | Garsivaz drives the moral disaster of the Siyavash cycle by poisoning Afrasiab's trust. His image should be human, courtly, and dangerous through speech, jealousy, and manipulation rather than demonic appearance. |
| Row 3, Col 5 | Arjasp / ارجاسپ | Goshtasp and Arjasp; Esfandiyar's war-cycle | The enemy king who attacks Goshtasp's realm. | Arjasp belongs to the Kayanian religious-political conflict around Goshtasp and Esfandiyar. His threat should be imperial and martial: raids, siege pressure, and war against the royal house Esfandiyar is commanded to defend. |
| Row 4, Col 1 | Pashang / پشنگ | Turanian royal genealogy; father of Afrasiab; Iran-Turan war-cycle | The elder Turanian king behind Afrasiab's line. | Pashang is important as dynastic background for Afrasiab's house rather than as a single monster encounter. His design should show old royal authority and the inherited Iran-Turan feud. |
| Row 4, Col 2 | Viseh / ویسه | House of Viseh in the Kay Khosrow war-cycle; Piran and Houman lineage | Elder of the Turanian noble house of Viseh. | Viseh anchors a noble Turanian family that includes morally complex figures such as Piran and war opponents such as Houman. He should be presented as aristocratic lineage and political weight, not as a creature. |
| Row 4, Col 3 | Houman / هومان | Kay Khosrow's wars with Turan; House of Viseh; Davazdah Rokh tradition | A Turanian champion from Piran's house. | Houman is a human battlefield opponent in the Iran-Turan wars. His description should reference noble Turanian armor, family loyalty, and the tragic pressure of fighting against Iranian champions. |
| Row 4, Col 4 | Barman / بارمان | Kay Khosrow's wars with Turan; Davazdah Rokh champion roster | A named Turanian front-line warrior. | Barman belongs to the roster of Turanian champions in the Kay Khosrow war material. Treat him as a human duelist or commander whose danger comes from trained force and battlefield honor. |
| Row 4, Col 5 | Nastihan / نستیهَن | Kay Khosrow's wars with Turan; House of Viseh / Turanian champion tradition | A young Turanian prince-warrior. | Nastihan should be represented as a royal or noble war figure from the Turanian side, not as a div. His Shahnameh role is best framed through the wider Iran-Turan battle cycle and the human cost of dynastic war. |
| Row 5, Col 1 | Biderafsh / بیدرفش | Kay Khosrow's wars with Turan; Turanian champion tradition | A named opposing champion in the Turanian wars. | Biderafsh functions in the game as a human champion from the opposing war roster. The description should keep him grounded in the Kay Khosrow war-cycle: armor, duel-readiness, and allegiance to the Turanian command structure. |
| Row 5, Col 2 | Shideh / شیده | Kay Khosrow's war against Afrasiab; Shideh son of Afrasiab | Afrasiab's son and royal battlefield opponent. | Shideh's significance comes from being Afrasiab's son, so his card should show young royal ambition, inheritance of the feud, and the pressure of fighting Kay Khosrow's avenging kingship. |
| Row 5, Col 3 | Kamus / کاموس | The Kamus-e Kushani campaign in Kay Khosrow's war-cycle | A mighty foreign/Turanian-allied warlord. | Kamus is a major campaign opponent in the heroic war material around Kay Khosrow and Rostam. His design should feel like a commanding warlord with heavy cavalry presence and the scale of a named campaign threat. |
| Row 5, Col 4 | Puladwand / پولادوند | Rostam's battles in the Kamus-e Kushani campaign | A steel-armored champion whose name evokes iron hardness. | Puladwand should be tied to the same war-cycle as Kamus, with "pulad" suggesting a hard, metal-armored visual identity. His danger is resistance, strength, and the grinding difficulty of defeating a heavily protected champion. |
| Row 5, Col 5 | Lahhak / لهاک | Afrasiab's line in Kay Khosrow's war-cycle | A Turanian royal warrior attached to Afrasiab's faction. | Lahhak is best treated as a human royal-war figure from Afrasiab's side rather than as a supernatural enemy. His description should show the darker end of dynastic loyalty within the Turanian court. |
| Row 6, Col 1 | Farshidvard / فرشیدورد | Afrasiab's line in Kay Khosrow's war-cycle | A Turanian royal warrior of Afrasiab's faction. | Farshidvard belongs to the human enemy roster around Afrasiab's house. His card should show noble armor and named-opponent dignity while keeping him inside the Kay Khosrow revenge war. |
| Row 6, Col 2 | Gorgin / گرگین | Bijan and Manijeh | The Iranian warrior whose betrayal traps Bijan. | Gorgin is morally gray rather than monstrous. In the Bijan and Manijeh story, his envy and false guidance lead Bijan into danger, so the image should show guilt, deceit, and an Iranian face shadowed by wrongdoing. |
| Row 6, Col 3 | Shaghad / شغاد | Rostam and Shaghad; Death of Rostam | Rostam's treacherous half-brother. | Shaghad's story is the death-trap of Rostam and Rakhsh: hidden pits, spears, and family betrayal. The design should be human and intimate in its treachery, because the horror is that Rostam falls to deception within his own kin. |
| Row 6, Col 4 | Salm / سلم | Fereydun's sons; murder of Iraj | The jealous brother who helps murder Iraj. | Salm's role is rooted in Fereydun's division of the world and the envy that follows. With Tur, he kills Iraj and begins a long vengeance cycle, so his card should show princely status stained by fratricide. |
| Row 6, Col 5 | Tur / تور | Fereydun's sons; murder of Iraj; origin of the Turanian feud | The jealous brother whose act seeds the Iran-Turan conflict. | Tur joins Salm in killing Iraj after Fereydun's division of realms. His image should connect fraternal violence to the later political identity of Turan and the epic feud that follows. |
