# Shahnameh Hero Atlas Ledger

Image: ![Hero atlas](../../src/assets/atlases/heroes-mythic-atlas.png)

Grid: 6 columns x 6 rows. Each image cell follows the exact position below.

Source note: story references name the Ferdowsi Shahnameh episode/cycle used for the short and detailed descriptions. Rows marked "brief mention" or "later epic tradition" should not be treated as full independent Ferdowsi story episodes.

| Position | Figure | Ferdowsi Shahnameh Story Reference | Short Description | Shahnameh-Based Description |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Row 1, Col 1 | Rostam / رستم | The Seven Labours of Rostam; Rostam and Sohrab; Rostam and Esfandiyar; Rostam and Shaghad | The central Sistani champion of the heroic age. | Rostam's identity comes from repeated Shahnameh trials: rescuing Kay Kavus in Mazandaran, unknowingly killing Sohrab, facing Esfandiyar under royal command, and dying through Shaghad's treachery. His image should carry the mace, Babr-e Bayan, Rakhsh, and the weight of a hero whose strength is tied to tragedy. |
| Row 1, Col 2 | Zal / زال | Birth of Zal; Zal and Rudabeh; Birth of Rostam | The white-haired son of Sam, raised by Simurgh. | Zal is exposed because of his white hair, rescued and raised by Simurgh, then returned to Sam. In the Rudabeh story and Rostam's birth, his bond with Simurgh becomes practical wisdom and healing aid, so his portrait should emphasize white hair, the feather, and elder heroic counsel. |
| Row 1, Col 3 | Simurgh / سیمرغ | Birth of Zal; Birth of Rostam; Rostam and Esfandiyar | The mythic bird who protects Zal's line. | Simurgh is not a human hero but a guardian force in the Shahnameh: she raises Zal, teaches how Rudabeh can survive Rostam's birth, and later guides Zal/Rostam in the Esfandiyar crisis. The image should show prophecy, healing, and mountain wisdom. |
| Row 1, Col 4 | Kaveh / کاوه | Zahhak, Fereydun, and Kaveh the Blacksmith | The blacksmith who raises revolt against Zahhak. | Kaveh enters Zahhak's court to demand justice for his sons and turns his leather apron into the rebel standard. His card should be grounded in the Zahhak episode: forge, hammer, popular rage, and the birth of the Derafsh-e Kaviani. |
| Row 1, Col 5 | Fereydun / فریدون | Zahhak and Fereydun; Fereydun's Sons: Salm, Tur, and Iraj | The just king who overthrows Zahhak. | Fereydun defeats Zahhak with the ox-headed mace and binds him in the mountain prison, restoring justice after Jamshid's fall. His later division of the world among Salm, Tur, and Iraj starts another tragic cycle, so his design should mix victory with the burden of kingship. |
| Row 1, Col 6 | Jamshid / جمشید | Reign and Fall of Jamshid; Prelude to Zahhak | The glory-king whose pride costs him the farr. | Jamshid is the world-ordering king associated with crafts, social order, and royal radiance, but his arrogance causes him to lose divine glory. His description should reference the Shahnameh's rise-and-fall pattern: brilliance first, then the opening for Zahhak. |
| Row 2, Col 1 | Hushang / هوشنگ | Reign of Hushang; Discovery of Fire and Sadeh | The early king linked with fire and civilization. | Hushang's key Shahnameh moment is discovering fire when a stone strikes another stone while he attacks a serpent. His portrait should describe the beginning of useful fire, early kingship, and the civilizing order of the Pishdadian line. |
| Row 2, Col 2 | Tahmuras / تهمورث | Reign of Tahmuras; Binding the Divs and Learning Writing | The king who subdues divs and gains written arts. | Tahmuras is remembered for mastering the divs; in return, they teach him scripts and knowledge. His design should refer to command over demonic forces and the disciplined acquisition of writing, not only brute magical power. |
| Row 2, Col 3 | Siyavash / سیاوش | Siyavash and Sudabeh; Trial by Fire; Siyavash in Turan | The innocent prince who proves purity through fire. | Siyavash rides through fire to refute Sudabeh's accusation, then enters Turan and is killed after court intrigue. His card should be based on the fire ordeal and later martyrdom: purity, a black horse, royal exile, and tragic innocence. |
| Row 2, Col 4 | Key Khosrow / کیخسرو | Birth and Finding of Kay Khosrow; War with Afrasiab; Kay Khosrow's Departure | The son of Siyavash who avenges his father. | Kay Khosrow is born in Turan after Siyavash's murder, protected by Piran, found by Giv, and brought to Iran. His rule culminates in revenge against Afrasiab and a mysterious spiritual departure, so his image should show visionary kingship and judgment. |
| Row 2, Col 5 | Esfandiyar / اسفندیار | Goshtasp and Esfandiyar; Battle of Rostam and Esfandiyar | The near-invulnerable prince trapped by duty. | Esfandiyar's Shahnameh tragedy is that obedience to Goshtasp drives him into conflict with Rostam. His "brazen" body and arrow fate should be described through sacred duty, invulnerability, and the impossible command to bind the Sistani hero. |
| Row 2, Col 6 | Gordafarid / گردآفرید | Rostam and Sohrab: Sohrab at the White Fortress / Dez-e Safid | The woman warrior who confronts Sohrab. | Gordafarid arms herself when Sohrab attacks the White Fortress guarded by Hojir. She fights him directly and delays the enemy by courage and wit, so her description should emphasize armor, spear, fortress defense, and active battlefield agency. |
| Row 3, Col 1 | Giv / گیو | Giv's Search for Kay Khosrow | The Iranian champion who finds Kay Khosrow. | Giv's major Shahnameh role is the long search that brings Kay Khosrow and Farangis from Turan to Iran. His image should carry the feeling of a rescue mission across dangerous terrain, not simply a generic scout. |
| Row 3, Col 2 | Goodarz / گودرز | Goodarz's Dream of Kay Khosrow; Davazdah Rokh | Elder strategist of the House of Goodarz. | Goodarz receives the dream that reveals Kay Khosrow's existence and later leads the Iranian side in the climactic wars, including Davazdah Rokh. The description should stress counsel, lineage, command, and old warrior intelligence. |
| Row 3, Col 3 | Bijan / بیژن | Bijan and Manijeh | The young warrior trapped in Turan and rescued. | Bijan's story begins with a boar-hunt mission and becomes a romance and captivity tale after Gorgin abandons him. His card should reference the pit, Manijeh's loyalty, and Rostam's eventual rescue. |
| Row 3, Col 4 | Manijeh / منیژه | Bijan and Manijeh | The Turanian princess who saves Bijan. | Manijeh defies court danger to feed and protect Bijan after he is imprisoned. Her description should come from that story: courage, loyalty across enemy lines, and active rescue rather than passive beauty. |
| Row 3, Col 5 | Farangis / فرنگیس | Siyavash in Turan; Birth and Rescue of Kay Khosrow | The Turanian princess who preserves Siyavash's line. | Farangis marries Siyavash in Turan and becomes the mother of Kay Khosrow after Siyavash is killed. Her role is survival, royal continuity, and the dangerous passage back to Iran with Giv. |
| Row 3, Col 6 | Sam / سام | Birth of Zal; Zal and Rudabeh | The elder Sistani champion, father of Zal. | Sam's defining Shahnameh role is his shame and later repentance over Zal's white hair, followed by reconciliation with the child saved by Simurgh. His image should carry ancestral authority and the beginning of the Sistani hero line. |
| Row 4, Col 1 | Nariman / نریمان | Sistani Genealogy in the Sam-Zal-Rostam Line; brief mention | Ancestor of the Sistani champions. | Nariman is important chiefly as a lineage figure in Ferdowsi's Shahnameh rather than the subject of a full independent episode. His description should present him as ancestral weight behind Sam, Zal, and Rostam, with any expanded exploits treated as later epic tradition. |
| Row 4, Col 2 | Garshasp / گرشاسپ | Early Heroic Genealogy; brief mention in Ferdowsi | Ancient monster-fighter of Iranian heroic memory. | Garshasp is present in the larger Iranian epic tradition, but Ferdowsi gives him only brief genealogical or early-heroic treatment compared with later Garshasp-nameh material. The card should mark him as a remembered ancient champion, not invent a full Ferdowsi episode. |
| Row 4, Col 3 | Bahram Gur / بهرام گور | Reign of Bahram Gur in the Sasanian Section | The hunter-king of the Sasanian stories. | Bahram Gur's Shahnameh material belongs to the historical/Sasanian age and emphasizes royal hunting, rulership, and testing of character. The description should reference the hunter-king persona rather than placing him in the earlier Rostam cycle. |
| Row 4, Col 4 | Shirin / شیرین | Reign of Khosrow Parviz; Khosrow and Shirin material in the Sasanian Section | A royal Sasanian woman tied to Khosrow Parviz. | Shirin belongs to the late Sasanian narrative world around Khosrow Parviz. Her description should show courtly influence, royal presence, and the political-romantic context of Khosrow's reign, not a generic princess. |
| Row 4, Col 5 | Rakhsh / رخش | Rostam Chooses Rakhsh; The Seven Labours; Rostam and Shaghad | Rostam's black, battle-tested stallion. | Rakhsh is Rostam's partner through the major heroic trials: chosen for unmatched strength, fighting beside him in Mazandaran, and dying with him in Shaghad's trap. The description should treat Rakhsh as an active heroic companion. |
| Row 4, Col 6 | Sohrab / سهراب | Rostam and Sohrab | Rostam's son and the young tragic champion. | Sohrab enters Iran seeking his father and is manipulated by the larger Iran-Turan conflict. His description should come from the recognition tragedy: youth, strength, hope, and the fatal ignorance between father and son. |
| Row 5, Col 1 | Tahmineh / تهمینه | Rostam and Sohrab: Rostam in Samangan | Princess of Samangan and mother of Sohrab. | Tahmineh approaches Rostam in Samangan and becomes Sohrab's mother. Her Shahnameh role should be described through royal initiative, the birth of the tragic child, and the token meant to identify Sohrab. |
| Row 5, Col 2 | Rudabeh / رودابه | Zal and Rudabeh; Birth of Rostam | Princess of Kabul, mother of Rostam. | Rudabeh's story is the romance with Zal and the extraordinary birth of Rostam aided by Simurgh's instruction. Her description should emphasize Kabul, long hair, love across political boundaries, and the origin of the greatest hero. |
| Row 5, Col 3 | Piran Viseh / پیران ویسه | Siyavash in Turan; Protection of Kay Khosrow; Davazdah Rokh | The wise Turanian noble who protects Kay Khosrow. | Piran is one of the Shahnameh's most morally complex Turanians: he shelters Siyavash's line yet remains loyal to Turan. His description should preserve that complexity and reference his eventual role in the Iran-Turan wars. |
| Row 5, Col 4 | Kay Kavus / کیکاووس | Kay Kavus's Mazandaran Expedition; Siyavash and Sudabeh; Kay Kavus Tries to Fly | A powerful but reckless Kayanian king. | Kay Kavus drives several Shahnameh crises: the disastrous Mazandaran campaign, the court tensions around Siyavash, and the famous failed attempt to fly. His description should show grandeur mixed with rash judgment. |
| Row 5, Col 5 | Tus / توس | The Forud Episode; Kay Khosrow's Wars with Turan; Davazdah Rokh | A fierce Iranian commander under Kay Khosrow. | Tus is a major battlefield commander whose harsh decisions help drive the tragedy of Forud and later wars with Turan. His description should reflect command strength and danger from impulsive pride. |
| Row 5, Col 6 | Faramarz / فرامرز | Rostam and Shaghad / Death of Rostam; brief Shahnameh role, expanded in later Faramarz-nameh | Rostam's son, remembered as a Sistani archer-warrior. | Ferdowsi's Shahnameh gives Faramarz a limited role compared with later epic expansions, especially around Rostam's death and revenge tradition. The card should reference him as Rostam's son and Sistani heir while noting that many adventures belong to post-Ferdowsi material. |
| Row 6, Col 1 | Arash / آرش | Manuchehr-Afrasiyab Border Tradition; allusive rather than full Ferdowsi episode | The legendary archer of the border. | Arash is essential to Iranian mythic memory, but Ferdowsi does not give him a full narrative episode like Rostam or Siyavash. The card should be marked as an allusive Shahnameh/traditional border figure, centered on the arrow that defines Iran's frontier. |
| Row 6, Col 2 | Hojir / هجیر | Rostam and Sohrab: Hojir at the White Fortress / Dez-e Safid | Gatekeeper champion captured by Sohrab. | Hojir guards the White Fortress when Sohrab arrives. His defeat and refusal to reveal Rostam's identity belong directly to the Sohrab tragedy, so his description should stress gatekeeping, loyalty, and the fatal secrecy around Rostam. |
| Row 6, Col 3 | Roham / رهام | Kay Khosrow's Wars with Turan; Davazdah Rokh tradition | Iranian cavalry hero in the Turanian wars. | Roham appears among the Iranian champions of the Kay Khosrow war-cycle. His description should tie him to mounted pursuit, battlefield service, and the larger Iran-Turan conflict rather than a separate romance tale. |
| Row 6, Col 4 | Pashotan / پشوتن | Goshtasp and Esfandiyar; War with Arjasp | Esfandiyar's righteous brother and defender. | Pashotan belongs to the Goshtasp-Arjasp-Esfandiyar cycle and stands for loyalty to the Kayanian order. His description should show defense, piety, and steadiness beside the more tragic Esfandiyar. |
| Row 6, Col 5 | Iraj / ایرج | Fereydun Divides the World; Murder of Iraj by Salm and Tur | The innocent son whose murder begins a vengeance cycle. | Iraj receives Iran from Fereydun and is killed by his jealous brothers Salm and Tur. His card should refer to innocence, kinship grief, and the origin of the Manuchehr revenge cycle. |
| Row 6, Col 6 | Banu Goshasp / بانو گشسب | Later Sistani epic tradition; not a full Ferdowsi Shahnameh episode | A woman pahlavan from the wider Sistani heroic cycle. | Banu Goshasp is important in the broader Persian heroic tradition but should be flagged as later epic material rather than a full Ferdowsi episode. Her description can support the game roster, but the ledger must state that her detailed adventures belong outside the core Shahnameh text. |
